Dynamic Inventory Management

Introduction

Organizing shelves in large-scale supermarkets is a critical factor in enhancing both consumer experience and operational efficiency. The placement of products directly influences customer behavior, product visibility, and, ultimately, sales performance. This thesis explores the development of an innovative recommendation system that optimizes shelf arrangement through reinforcement learning techniques. Specifically, Q-learning and epsilon-greedy algorithms were employed to construct the model. These algorithms address key challenges of exploration and exploitation, which are fundamental to reinforcement learning, particularly in environments with dynamic and large scale datasets like supermarkets.

Traditionally, reinforcement learning has been underutilized for large-scale datasets in retail due to these challenges. However, this work demonstrates its feasibility and effectiveness by simulating the supermarket environment. Through repeated learning cycles, the proposed system efficiently learned and adapted optimal shelf organization strategies, which contributed to more effective product placement and improved overall store performance.

Adding a fresh perspective to the very small amount of research on reinforcement learning applications in large-scale retail settings, this thesis tackles the challenges posed by dynamic customer behavior and shifting product demand. The accomplishment of this research paves the path for additional investigation into machine learning techniques to improve operational decision making in the retail industry, opening the door to more sophisticated, automated systems for shop management.

Research Problem & Solution

Proposed Problem

The research problem revolves around the challenges of using Q-learning in large-scale supermarket inventory management systems, where the complexities of handling vast data volumes and dynamic inventory operations make traditional Q-learning approaches insufficient. While Q-learning has been widely applied in smaller settings, its applicability to larger supermarkets is limited by its inability to efficiently scale, particularly when addressing the exploration-exploitation dilemma inherent in reinforcement learning. This issue is further compounded by the need to optimize shelf organization in these complex environments, making it necessary to rethink existing frameworks to better manage the inventory of large supermarkets.


Proposed Solution

The proposed solution focuses on developing a hybrid model that integrates Q-learning which can handle exploration-exploitation with combining the algorithms, namely epsilon-greedy, Upper Confidence Bound (UCB), and Thompson sampling. By combining these approaches, the research aims to mitigate the shortcomings of traditional Q-learning and enhance decision-making in dynamic, large-scale supermarket environments. A hybrid approach was tested, leading to an improved recommendation system for shelf organization with a significant accuracy rate of 62%. Further refinement of the model, incorporating epsilon-greedy adjustments, resulted in a 90% accuracy, making it highly effective in optimizing shelf layouts while ensuring scalability and robustness in handling large datasets by handling exploration and exploitation.

Research Objectives

Optimizing Inventory Management in Large-Scale Supermarkets Using Enhanced Q-Learning Techniques

Aiming at addressing the longstanding problem of successfully handling the large data sets and navigating the dilemmas during the exploration-exploitation stage of Qlearning, this research paper presents an integrated solution that significantly improves the current inventory management systems of the large-scale supermarkets.

Design and Implement Q-learning Algorithm for Large-Scale Supermarket Inventory Recommendation Systems

This sub-objective is about the careful design and execution of a Q learning algorithm that will (with flexibility and precision) match the complex nature of operations in large-scale supermarkets. Using novel algorithmic design approaches and optimization techniques as a basis, the strategic goal becomes the development of a robust system architecture which dynamically adjusts to varying market scenarios as well as massive data processing.

Develop a Dashboard for the Recommendation System

While developing the Q-learning AI, the second objective is to design an interactive dashboard that communicates with the AI through an easy-to-use interface. The dashboard becomes an essential tool in the hands of all the stakeholders who need realtime data about inventory recommendations that are believed to be generated by the Q-learning algorithm. Integration of immersive visualization and extensive data analytics functions, aims at equipping decision-makers with useful data-driven information resources, which will consequently help for the implementation of informed decision-making and performance enhancement across supermarket environments.

Methodology

This research presents a methodology for optimizing shelf organization in supermarkets using a hybrid Q-learning approach to tackle the exploration-exploitation dilemma in reinforcement learning. The study first reviews the limitations of traditional Q-learning, especially its tendency to get trapped in suboptimal policies when handling large datasets. It then evaluates three specific algorithms: epsilon-greedy, Upper Confidence Bound (UCB), and Thompson sampling, which offer different strategies to manage exploration and exploitation.

Epsilon-greedy predominantly exploits known information while occasionally exploring new actions. UCB applies an optimistic approach when uncertain, assuming unknown actions may yield higher rewards. Thompson sampling balances exploiting known strategies with exploring new actions to gather more information.

These algorithms were tested in a recommendation system for shelf organization, with UCB and Thompson sampling reaching 49% accuracy, while epsilon-greedy achieved 5%. A hybrid model combining elements from all three algorithms was then proposed, leading to an improvement, reaching 62% accuracy.

An improved hybrid model, incorporating principles from Q-learning and epsilon-greedy, further refined the methodology. Through multiple iterations and testing, the hybrid model demonstrated an accuracy of 90% in shelf optimization, outperforming both traditional Q-learning and individual algorithms. This approach allows for better exploration of product placement possibilities, ensuring efficient exploitation of learned policies. The model was validated through simulations and real-world tests, showing its adaptability to various shelving configurations. This research demonstrates that combining Q-learning with the epsilon-greedy strategy significantly enhances decision-making in dynamic environments like supermarkets.
