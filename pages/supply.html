Supply Chain Optimization

Introduction

In this current study, a research paper is developed to propose an innovative approach to supply chain management, which involves the Random Forest Classification and Fuzzy Logic Hybrid Model. The suggested method intends to improve the decision-making process of selecting the most efficient suppliers by integrating the attributes of the Random Forest model with the features of the fuzzy logic system. The model is evaluated on a dataset obtained from a supermarket’s supplier database.

The measurements used are precision, recall, and the f1-score. These also show that the improvement brought by the hybrid model is significant in terms of accuracy and f lexibility when compared with traditional machine learning techniques, especially when dealing with vague data. Further, this paper explains the implementation details of the different models, how to evaluate them, and their applicability in real-life supply chain management.

Research Gap

  1. Current supply chain research in supply chain optimization has mainly concerned itself with the task of finding the optimal supplier under ordinary conditions by the use of tools such as clustering and machine learning models. These approaches have been helpful in organizations wherein the supply chain processes are steady. Yet, the global COVID-19 pandemic revealed various concerns over conventional supply chain ecosystems since the suppliers and businesses found it extremely challenging to manage product availability and delivery effectiveness during this period. The pandemic made it clear that a reserving supply chain development approach sufficiently using widely available tactical disciplines is required for resilience in crisis situations and disruptions.
  2. Although there are still many papers and articles acknowledging the current models’ imperfections and the requirement for crisis-adaptive solutions, few reliable supplier selection models are available that can address the challenges of a crisis. In general, most of the models are developed for conventional environment, and sufficiently obvious is the recognized shortage of information considering crisis management aspects as an integral part of supplier selection procedures. This is a good opportunity for a transition towards developing a more ‘robust’ or ‘dual context’ model a system that is efficient under ‘routine’ conditions, and which can cope better during crises.

Research Problem & Solution

Proposed Problem

The COVID-19 outbreak revealed major flaws in current supplier selection models meant for consistent market conditions. Traditional supply chain models, such as clustering algorithms and machine learning (ML) methods, centered on enhancing efficiency and performance by analyzing past data and regular patterns. Nevertheless, these models did not adjust well to the unpredictability and interruptions brought about by the pandemic, resulting in scarcities and disruptions in the supply chain. The main problem is that these models are unable to consider varying supplier performance or manage unclear, insufficient, or vague data in times of crisis. Consequently, there is a requirement for a supplier selection model that is more robust and can operate effectively in both routine and emergency scenarios.


Proposed Solution

The solution is a hybrid model that integrates the predictive power of Random Forest (RF) with the f lexibility of Fuzzy Logic (FL). The RF model analyzes historical data to make initial predictions about supplier performance, while the FL system refines these predictions by handling uncertainty and qualitative factors like supplier reputation or delivery delays. If the RF model’s predictions are uncertain, the FL system applies expert-driven fuzzy rules to adjust the evaluation. This combination ensures accurate and reliable supplier selection under both routine and emergency scenarios, making supply chains more resilient and adaptable to disruptions.

Research Objectives

A Comprehensive AI-Based Framework for Supplier Selection in Supply Chain Management

The framework for selecting the best supplier in supply chain management means a combination of methods: data collection and strategies, preprocessing, model building, decision making incorporating artificial intelligence and fuzzy logic, and evaluation of performance. All of these steps have been incorporated in a very detailed and thoughtful way to produce an accurate, interpretable, and stable supplier selection model.

Data Collection and Preprocessing

Data from Spar Supermarket in Sri Lanka, covering transactional records, assessed supplier efficiency using variables like product type, item count, quality, delivery dates, location, and ratings. Mode imputation addressed missing data, while feature extraction calculated delivery delay for supplier ratings. The dataset was split 80:20 for training and testing to evaluate model generalization, with the refined data used to develop a fuzzy logic model.

Fuzzy Logic Model

The Fuzzy Logic (FL) model enhanced supplier selection by incorporating human logic through fuzzy rules and triangular membership functions for input variables like quantity, delivery delay, quality, geolocation, and risk management. These functions transformed crisp inputs into fuzzy sets (e.g., low, medium, high), accommodating data uncertainties. The model used fuzzy rules based on field knowledge to infer supplier ratings, which were then defuzzified to produce a final, concrete rating for supplier performance.

Random Forest Model

The Random Forest (RF) model was developed using an 80:20 split for training and testing, with 100 estimators to capture feature interactions and predict supplier ratings accurately. It categorized suppliers into five-star ratings while providing insights into variable importance, enhancing interpretability. The model's performance was evaluated using precision, recall, and F1-score metrics, which validated its ability to correctly classify suppliers and assess rating accuracy across different levels.

Hybrid Model

The RF-FL hybrid model combines Random Forest and Fuzzy Logic to enhance supplier selection efficiency and interpretability. Initially, the RF model predicts supplier ratings using structured data, followed by refinement through the FL model, which applies fuzzy rules to incorporate expert knowledge and address data subtleties. This integration improves prediction accuracy while enriching decision-making attributes. The iterative refinement allows human interpretation to influence the model, making the final supplier ratings more reliable and grounded.

Evaluation Metrics

The hybrid RF-FL model's performance was assessed using precision, recall, F1-score, and a confusion matrix. Precision measured correct positive predictions, while recall evaluated the detection rate of true positives. The F1-score provided a balance between precision and recall. The confusion matrix highlighted prediction errors. Evaluating both the RF and RF-FL models showed that the hybrid approach significantly improved accuracy and F1-scores, especially in complex data scenarios.

Methodology

D. Hybrid Model

The present RF-FL model is developed with the combination of the Random Forest model and the Fuzzy Logic model to improve both efficiency and understanding of the supplier selection. In the hybrid approach, therefore, the use of the RF model is inestimable in arriving at the initial prediction of the supplier rating by using the structured data. The refined forecast is thus obtained by the FL model which imposes fuzzy rules to capture the expert knowledge and to deal with subtleties of the data not handled well by the RF model alone. This sequential integration implies that the final supplier rating avails of the enhanced accuracy of the RF model, at the same time, endows the supplier decision with rich decision-making attributes that the FL model harnesses. Because of the iterative improvement of the supplier rating as reflected from the FL model, the refinement of the predictions made in the RF model is possible. This way, the proposal of new values based on fuzzy logic rules ‘incorporates’ human interpretation into the model, making the supplier ratings the hybrid model derives even more grounded and reliable.
